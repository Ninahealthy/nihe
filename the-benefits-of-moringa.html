Health Benefits Of Moringa

Moringa appears to be a fantastic molecule to activate an enzyme called NRF2.

NRF2 has been identified as a master regulator of the antioxidant response, due to its regulation of many downstream genes involved in counterbalancing oxidative stress, so in a nutshell NRF2 it activates a whole battery of cell cascades and pathways that protect our cells against oxidants, and there's a group of molecules called isothiocyanates, which are well studied for activating NRF2 and it's thought that these isothiocyanates are responsible for the health benefits of cruciferous vegetables, and there are some limited studies suggesting that higher intakes of cruciferous vegetables reduces the risk of lung bladder and colorectal cancer and among these different isothiocyanates.

sulforaphane has been identified as one of the most potent activators of NRF2, however although sulforaphane has been extensively studied its chemical instability poses challenges, and one alternative is to use a group of stable isothiocyanates that's derived from moringa, overall sulforaphane looks to be a great molecule to activate NRF2 and activate our antioxidant defenses, the trouble is sulforaphane is highly unstable, so if you take a supplement in by the time you take that supplement most of the sulforaphane would have broken down and that's where moringa comes in, so moringa also looks to be a great way of activating NRF2, but it's far more stable compared to sulforaphane.

Clinical trials

When we have a look at the mice trials moringa, seems to give anti-inflammatory and antioxidant activity, and also helps with diseases such as ulcerative colitis and reduces insulin resistance in mice fed a high-fat diet,

now that we've gone through the theory about moringa and some of the mice trials, let's have a look at what the human clinical data shows.

unfortunately while we do have a lot of animal studies, there's not many human published clinical trials especially those which include an adequate number of people, furthermore some of the methods in which the way these studies were carried out are missing.

Generally clinical studies they're done in people who have got some form of disease, this is to help power the studies, so if a particular molecule is going to be beneficial to already healthy people, then we should be seeing an even greater benefit in people that have got diseases, and because there might be more benefit in people that have got diseases, it's easier to detect that benefit in clinical trials, the only trials using moringa were in patients that have got diabetes.

let's have a look and see what those trials showed, we'll start by having a look at two studies that were performed in type 2 diabetic patients.

the first study had 22 diabetic patients and the second one had 60 patients, in the first trial there was a 26 reduction in blood sugar levels after eating a meal and a similar result was found in the second trial, where there was a 29 reduction in blood sugar levels and on the surface.

the final diabetic trial that i could find was one where they took 32 newly diagnosed type 2 diabetics, they gave half of the participants 4 grams of moringa and the other half placebo, unfortunately this trial showed no difference in either the fasting blood sugar levels HBA1C or the average blood sugar levels, those results are a bit disappointing for diabetic patients.

the only trial that i could find in healthy people, was this one where they took 60 post-menopausal healthy women, half of them had 7 grams of moringa and the other half had no supplements and they were followed up for three months, in this period a 13.5 reduction in blood sugar levels there also appeared to be improvements in antioxidant enzymes such as glutathione and superoxide dismutase.

Based on the studies described here the evidence in humans on the potential usefulness of moringa as an additional therapy to control high blood sugar levels is so far limited but promising.
